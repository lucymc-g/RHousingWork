Key Findings

Summary of Fair Lending Analysis

  • Across all Consortium jurisdictions, Hispanic applicants had the highest loan denial rates. Longmont had the most pronounced disparity at 20% for Hispanic applicants versus 9% for non-Hispanic White applicants.
  • In Boulder and Broomfield Counties, Hispanic applicants had higher denial rates than non-Hispanic White applicants at every income level — suggesting disparities are not fully explained by applicant qualifications and may reflect discriminatory factors.
  • High debt-to-income ratio was the most common denial reason across races in Boulder County, but Hispanic applicants who were denied had the highest proportion citing credit history as a reason (55%).
  • Hispanic applicants in Boulder County were more than twice as likely to receive a high-priced loan (3.2%) compared to non-Hispanic White applicants (1.5%). Black applicants had the highest rate at 4.2%, though the sample size was small (n=24).
  • Tract-level mapping of denial rates and high-priced loan rates was overlaid with HUD-defined Hispanic concentration areas to identify potential geographic fair lending patterns.

Data & Methods

Approach

Primary Data

HMDA 2023 loan-level records. Applications filtered to primary residence, owner-occupied properties.

Denial Rate

Defined as denied applications divided by all applications excluding withdrawals and incomplete files, consistent with Federal Reserve methodology.

High-Priced Loans

Loans with APR more than 1.5 percentage points above comparable treasuries. Not necessarily predatory; used to flag areas for scrutiny.

Race/Ethnicity

Denial rates computed by race and ethnicity, with sample size thresholds (n ≥ 20) applied. Small-n groups noted throughout.

Geographic Analysis

Tract-level denial and high-priced loan rates mapped in R using tmap, overlaid with HUD RECAP Hispanic concentration thresholds from ACS 5-year estimates.

Context

Produced for Boulder HOME Consortium Consolidated Plan and AFFH fair lending assessment.
